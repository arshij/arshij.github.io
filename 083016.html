A NEW PERSPECTIVE

Here's to a fresh start.

August 30, 2016

Going to one of the most competitive high schools in California was definitely difficult. I was immature, I complained about the problems instead of finding solutions, and I was pessimistic. Four years ago, I was a completely different person. It wasn't till the summer of 2016 that I truly understood myself, who I wanted to become, and how to become that person. I realized the mistakes I made in the past, the good and bad connections I made along the way, and I got a chance to change my perspective on... well, everything.

I recently finished reading an amazing book, Deep Work, by Cal Newport (a truly remarkable individual). After reading his take on "focused success in a distracted world", I realized how much time I've wasted in high school. I feel guilty, and I know many others who do, too.

To summarize, Newport breaks this book into two sections - why deep work is important, and how to achieve deep work. His rules for success are: work deeply, embrace boredom, quit social media, and drain the shallows. I highly recommend this book to anyone who wants an honest, straight-forward and life-changing read.

I am now learning to alter my study habits. In high school, I spent hours memorizing facts that were forgotten the day after the test was taken (ultimately forcing me to cram the same information again for finals). I spent hours doing busy work for classes I was not interested in with unreasonable teachers. I did not sleep; the past two years of my life, I have been surviving on 2 to 4 hours of a sleep a night to often not sleeping at all. The load became so much that I quit playing for my high school tennis team my senior year, after committing to the sport for twelve years. I procrastinated, I was always struggling to catch up, and I was unhealthy. I constantly told myself I needed to change my lifestyle, but I just never got the chance to stop and breathe.

Going to a high school where you are forced to perform higher than insanely competitive students who came from the same socioeconomic background as you was tough. I was burned out. I changed as a person; I don't know who I became. I desperately attempted to catch up on sleep whenever I could, and I'll admit - sometimes it was during class. I constantly told myself "pain is temporary, GPA is forever" and performed each task assigned to me solely because if I didn't I'd be behind. I understand a lot of students can relate to every aspect of this, and will blame it on their high school. That's what I did. I blamed my teachers, I blamed my peers, I blamed everyone but myself. And that was the wrong mindset. I was to blame the entire time.

I did not stop and think about what I was doing. I just kept going, not questioning anything or anyone, in fear of staying left behind. And that's where I went wrong. I should have stopped. I should have noticed something was wrong. But I won't dwell on the past. I finally had this summer of freedom, not worrying about SATs and ACTs or going to some life-changing summer program that I can write my college application essays about. And in this summer of freedom, I realized so many things.

    1. SLEEP
    I never truly understood the powerful impact of staying well-rested. You wake up with a glow on your face (instead of dark circles and eye bags like I have been for the past two years), you feel happy and confident, you are healthy, and you perform significantly better on all your tasks.

    2. GENUINE FRIENDSHIPS AND CONNECTIONS
    I got around to realize who I'm investing my time and effort in. I understood who was truly my friend, who I could go to in times of despair, who would show up for me when I needed them. I then proceeded to filter outÂ all of those individuals I met that have caused me to be negative, and I continued to converse with those who brought out the best in me. No more negativity and pessimism in my life.

    3. FUTURE CAREER PLANS
    It's never too early to start thinking about your future. I have decided to pursue an M.B.A. and/or M.S. in computer science after I graduate. I have created a plan expressing my academic and career-building aspirations. I have set high goals for myself, because as my favorite character Harvey Specter says, "I don't have dreams, I have goals". I've realized it's okay to dream big; failure is a part of life.

I can go on and on with this list, but these three things are what I've thought of most this summer. I am beyond overjoyed to be heading to college with such a powerful and strong mindset. I am excited to go to classes that I am genuinely interested in, with no more busy work to put something in the grade-book, and with professors that truly care about the subject they are teaching. I am passionate about my studies, and I am proud to say I have matured a great deal from the girl I was four years ago. I am ready for whatever college throws at me, which I only have Dougherty Valley High to thank, for transforming me into an aggressive, determined and confident young woman. My new perspective on life will carry me to achieving my goals.
